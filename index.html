My research examines how credentialing regimes, fiscal architectures, and governance reforms reorganize professional legitimacy and labor stratification in early childhood education. I analyze California’s Universal Transitional Kindergarten and PK–3 credential reforms as policy sites where institutional definitions of “qualification” redistribute recognition, authority, and workforce access.

Grounded in Critical Feminist Theory, Intersectionality, and Community Cultural Wealth, I employ critical phenomenology and cross-role qualitative inquiry to examine how educators, faculty, and district leaders interpret reform implementation. My work interrogates how equity-framed expansions can reproduce stratification through credential alignment, funding flows, and administrative control.

As a practicing early childhood educator, I situate policy analysis within lived institutional contexts, linking qualitative inquiry to institutional design and workforce equity debates.
